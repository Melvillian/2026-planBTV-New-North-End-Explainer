## 1. Executive Summary

### The headline numbers
- Burlington must produce **~7,000 new housing units by 2050 (~280/year)** per the Mayor's 2026 Housing Strategy (pp. 2, 8, 22–23). "Additional housing in the New North End is not a question of 'whether,' but 'how'" (p. 23).
- The plan identifies **five focus areas** whose full buildout adds **835 dwelling units and 279,000 SF of commercial space** (p. 15).
- The NNE has **~10,500 residents**, bounded by Lake Champlain and the Winooski River (p. 8). Population grew only 2% from 2000–2020 while the rest of the city grew 20% (p. 56).
- Median age 43 vs. 27 in the rest of the city; **21% of residents are over 65** — more than double the citywide share (p. 56). The NNE has the city's largest percentage of older adults (p. 28).
- **~350 acres across 10+ parks** — more open space than any other Burlington neighborhood (pp. 33, 54).
- Infrastructure headroom: full buildout adds ~143,780 gal/day of wastewater — the North Plant would go from ~40% to ~47% of its 2.0 MGD capacity, "well within design limits" (pp. 14, 52, 185).

### The core narrative
- The neighborhood "was built primarily for car travel, with a street network that funnels nearly all trips onto a single corridor — North Avenue... Growth cannot simply add more vehicles to an already constrained system" (p. 8). ~70% of residents drive to work, 61% alone — nearly 20 points higher than the rest of the city (p. 46).
- Vision statement (pp. 8, 30): "New North End residents envision their neighborhood growing to become a well-connected, inclusive, and vibrant community that balances growth with affordability, sustainability, and fosters a strong sense of place."
- Organizing concept: the **15-minute neighborhood** — most daily needs within a 15-minute walk or bike ride (pp. 9, 60, 64). The neighborhood is only 2 mi × 1.5 mi; nearly all of it is a 5-minute bike ride from North Ave (pp. 46, 50).
- Three interconnected frameworks (p. 9): **Transportation** (safe walking/biking/transit), **Urban Design** (cluster housing + mixed use at key North Ave locations), **Placemaking** (squares, plazas, gathering spaces). A framework is "not a project plan or a fixed set of rules" but a menu of strategies.
- Six guiding principles (pp. 9, 31): inclusive community · housing choice + affordability · safer connected transportation · walkable mixed-use neighborhood centers · protect natural spaces · sustainability + green infrastructure.

### Process & status
- Developed Dec 2024–June 2026 by the Office of City Planning with CCRPC; consultants Utile, Nelson/Nygaard, Landwise, Wagner Hodgson, Engineering Ventures (pp. 3, 8, 24). Draft dated "Adopted XX June 2026" (p. 1).
- Engagement: Project Advisory Committee (4 meetings), 2 public meetings, 2 open houses, 2 online surveys, focus groups, ~15 pop-up events (pp. 10, 26–27).
- Outcomes feed into the citywide comprehensive plan, planBTV 2050 (pp. 21, 32).
- Implementation: **44 actions** across 5 categories (Mobility M1–M14, TDM1–7, Greenway GW1–9, Urban Design UD1–8, Placemaking P1–6), each with timeframe (Immediate 1–2 yr / Short 2–5 / Medium 5–10 / Long 10+), lead agency, $–$$$ cost, and "Quick Win" flags (pp. 17–18, 207–219). "Implementation is not guaranteed" — it depends on resident participation, political will, and funding (p. 207).

## 2. Top 10 Development Locations

**Note:** The plan names **five focus areas**, not ten. Sites 6–10 below are secondary/supporting locations drawn from the corridor and transportation chapters if the website needs a list of ten. Focus areas were chosen via community input plus a "soft sites" analysis scoring parcels 0–6 on spatial potential, economic potential, and development opportunity; parcels with wetlands, forests, critical infrastructure, or significant existing housing were excluded (pp. 14, 68–69). All conceptual visions are "illustrative visions rather than prescriptive plans" requiring further study and outreach (p. 139).

### The five focus areas (full detail pp. 146–184; summary p. 15)

**1. Ethan Allen Plaza** — privately owned; the largest site. "NNE's 'Town Center'": **323 DU / ~249k SF commercial** (Option 1, p. 152). Keeps the Hannaford grocery; linear multi-use plaza from North Ave to Leddy Park; mixed-use 4-story garage; ground-floor retail; new bus stops and protected bike lanes (pp. 150–153). **Option 2** (response to feedback) phases the work and retains more existing businesses — Key Bank, Post Office, Ace Hardware stay in Phase 1 (177 DU), plaza + Leddy Park link in Phase 2 (265 DU) (pp. 154–159). UD6: masterplan is an Immediate Quick Win (pp. 216–217). Community: "broad support" — underutilized surface parking, avoids sensitive parkland; skepticism about the garage, small-business displacement, affordability (p. 158).

**2. Plattsburg Ave at North Ave** — privately owned. "Neighborhood Main Street and village square": **139 DU / ~16.4k SF retail** (p. 162). Mobility hub + café at Franklin Square, civic plazas both sides of North Ave, raised intersection, new park; housing from 4-story multifamily (under new RC zoning) down to townhomes/quads (pp. 160–163). Community: support for housing/plazas/cemetery "parkification"; concerns about displacement of single-family homes, views, character; requests for senior-friendly stair-free units (p. 164).

**3. North Ave Alliance Church** — privately owned. Neighborhood gateway at the VT 127 interchange: **138 DU** (p. 148). Modern roundabout, 3-over-1 multifamily facing North Ave, townhomes on a shared-use street, mobility hub + civic plaza (café/bike shop/visitor center), two pedestrian paths into Arms Forest (pp. 146–149). Community: support for gateway/roundabout/bike design; concerns about congestion, Arms Forest recreation pressure, density (p. 148).

**4. Ethan Allen Parkway** — City-owned (park land). New neighborhood center via a **90-degree bend of the Parkway at North Ave**: **123 DU / ~14.8k SF retail** envisioned as "restaurants, breweries, and cultural venues" (pp. 174–177). Ethan Allen Gateway Plaza linking park to street, community garden, community center, playground preserved. Community: strongest opposition of any site — historic park entrance, grass lawn, habitat, ephemeral plants; residents "generally not in favor of using the front lawn of Ethan Allen Park for housing" (pp. 107, 178). UD7 study is an Immediate Quick Win; plan urges "a cautious and deliberate approach" to housing in parks (pp. 178, 216).

**5. Starr Farm West (Starr Farm Park)** — City-owned (park land). "Enhanced park gateway": **111–113 DU** (pp. 15, 182). Greenway mobility hub + café with bike repair, new bike path Greenway→North Ave, splash pad + ice rink, community gardens, soccer field upgrades, concession stand; **multigenerational/senior housing** with accessible entries and elevators (pp. 180–183). Community: supporters like housing diversity + retail; opponents cite tree canopy/habitat loss ("rare and possibly threatened bird and plant species" along Starr Farm Rd), stormwater, distance from services (p. 184).

### Secondary/supporting locations (to reach ten)

**6. City-owned land generally** — UD1 Public Asset Study of every City asset's development feasibility; UD2 explore public land for housing in planBTV 2050 (both Immediate Quick Wins, pp. 216–217). Using City land can cut housing costs "by as much as 20 percent" and generate perpetual revenue via ground leases (pp. 166–168).
**7. 1024, 1014 & 996 North Ave** — parcels tied to the "Bend Ethan Allen" intersection concept; City ROW could be combined with these parcels to incentivize redevelopment (PDF pp. 106–108).
**8. North Avenue Residential Corridor (RC) zone** — the corridor spine itself; RC zoning enables multifamily up to 4 stories with ground-floor retail along "much of North Ave" (pp. 39, 65, 160).
**9. Trail-oriented development along the Burlington Greenway** — homes near the path with cafés, bike shops, repair stations serving path users (PDF p. 130).
**10. Cambrian Rise / Burlington High School area** — existing growth precedent (2017–) served by North Ave Phase 5 reconstruction (Neighborhood Connector Zone) (pp. 23, 37, PDF p. 119).

### Buildings proposed (the "Kit of Parts," pp. 141–145)
Six typologies, max four stories: 3-over-1 multifamily (retail below, 3 floors homes above), single-stair multifamily (≤12 units), sixplex ("missing middle... gentle transition to existing single-family"), neighborhood multifamily (2–4 units), townhouse, and community facility.

## 3. Impact on Existing Residents

### Zoning that already changed / will change
- The **Neighborhood Code** (adopted early 2024) rezoned all NNE residential areas to massing-based rules; RL/RM zones now allow **duplexes, triplexes, quadplexes, and cottage courts** on residential streets; the **Residential Corridor (RC)** zone along North Ave allows up to 4-story multifamily with ground-floor commercial (pp. 39–40, 65, 160). The plan says this "is not the end of zoning reform in the NNE" — further adjustments follow, incl. design guidelines (P1) and zoning analysis for Ethan Allen Parkway/Starr Farm PUD standards (pp. 17, 32, 186).
- Maximum proposed building scale anywhere is **four stories**; frameworks use "gentle transition" typologies next to single-family homes (pp. 141–143, 160).

### Traffic, driving, and parking
- North Avenue **keeps its existing 2–3 vehicle travel lanes** under the preferred redesign (pp. 11, 92) and **adds** on-street parking/loading on the northeast side (p. 92, 97).
- New development generates **under 3,000 net new daily vehicle trips** — a ~30% increase over North Ave's current ~10,000 vehicles/day, within the corridor's 10,000–15,000/day capacity, though peak-period intersection pressure is possible (PDF pp. 124–125).
- Drivers will encounter **roundabouts** (VT 127 interchange preferred; Ethan Allen Pkwy possible; Institute Rd explored) (PDF pp. 103, 107, 119).
- Side-street changes: Tracy Dr/Loaldo Dr proposed one-way pair at North Ave; Saratoga Ave one-way-in explored; Gazo Ave strategic closures/directional changes explored; driveways consolidated/removed along North Ave as redevelopment occurs (PDF pp. 80, 101, 111, 116–117).
- Traffic calming (chicanes, speed humps, raised intersections, neighborhood traffic circles) on designated quiet streets, with community participation in design (PDF pp. 76, 84).
- Property impact note: the *rejected* Plattsburg roundabout would have required ROW from two private parcels and demolition of a Burlington Housing Authority house — a key reason "Bend North Ave" was preferred instead (PDF p. 111).

### Affordability and staying in the neighborhood
- Housing-choice principle: affordable and mixed-income options so the NNE "remains accessible to families, older adults, and lower-income residents" (pp. 8–9). Residents emphasized homeownership for young families over high-rent apartments (p. 28).
- City-owned land strategy: land is 10–20% of project cost, so public land can cut costs "by as much as 20 percent"; the City can require affordability, prioritize land trusts/co-ops/nonprofits, and never sell the land (ground leases) (pp. 166–168, 188). CHIP financing pathway (UD4) and PPP template (UD5) are Immediate actions (p. 17, 216).
- Aging in place (AARP study, p. 29): 68% of older adults say rising property taxes affect their ability to stay "a lot"; 57% rate senior housing options fair/poor; 64% worry about when they can no longer drive. Starr Farm West's senior housing and the plan's transit/walkability agenda respond directly.
- Displacement concerns are recorded as community feedback at Plattsburg (single-family homes/residents) and Ethan Allen Plaza (small businesses) (pp. 158, 164); Option 2 at the Plaza was created specifically to retain more existing businesses (p. 154).

### Services and daily life
- Route 7 bus: restore Sunday service (currently none) as a near-term priority, increase frequency as the neighborhood grows, shelters/benches at warranted stops (pp. 13, 120–121, 210–211).
- Water mains along North Ave (some from the 1920s) must be replaced **as a prerequisite for development** (UD8, Long-term, $$$) — protecting service for "new and existing residents alike" (pp. 14, 185, 188, 216).
- Wastewater: North Plant has ample capacity (40% used now, ~47% after full buildout) (pp. 14, 52, 185).
- If public open space is developed for housing, the City must **replace commensurate acreage and programming elsewhere** (UD3, Immediate Quick Win) (pp. 17, 187, 216).
- What happens first: nearly all Urban Design actions (UD1–UD7) are Immediate (1–2 yr) *studies and plans*, not construction; construction of corridor phases is Short→Long term (pp. 208–219).
- Resident voice going forward: NNE mobility advisory committee (TDM5), annual mode-share survey (TDM6), Neighborhood Placemaking Committee (P2) (pp. 212, 218).

## 4. Improvements to New North End

### North Avenue redesign (the centerpiece)
- Preferred design (scored 23/24 vs. alternatives): **two-way separated bike lane/shared-use path** on the southwest side with crossing-free Greenway access, widened sidewalks (10–15'), in-lane bus stops, on-street parking/loading, 6'+ green buffers with rain gardens and trees (also fixing existing flooding), dedicated bike signals and raised crossings (pp. 11, 92–97).
- Built in **five phases**, south to north, each starting with a scoping study (PDF pp. 114–119): Phase 1 Parkway Zone (Institute Rd–Killarney Dr, Short-term); Phase 2 Main Street Zone (Killarney–Gosse Ct, Medium); Phase 3 Neighborhood Main Street (Gosse Ct–Plattsburg, Medium); Phase 4 Neighborhood Slow Street (Plattsburg–Northview, Long); Phase 5 Neighborhood Connector (Institute Rd–Washington St, Long).

### Three intersection transformations
- **North Ave / VT 127 interchange**: Dutch-style modern roundabout with continuous bike path, raised crossings, gateway landscaping and public art; leftover highway space returned to native ecology (PDF pp. 98–103).
- **North Ave / Ethan Allen Parkway**: roundabout vs. "Bend Ethan Allen" 90° realignment — no preferred option yet, pending more engagement (2020 study ended "no build" after resident opposition) (PDF pp. 72, 104–109).
- **North Ave / Plattsburg Ave**: preferred "Bend North Ave" 90° realignment (22/24) with raised Franklin Square intersection, new public plaza and mobility hub (PDF pp. 110–113).

### Biking and walking network
- Three family-friendly facility types: separated bike lanes, shared-use paths, neighborhood greenways on quiet streets (PDF pp. 74–76).
- **Neighborhood Greenway Loop** (M1, Short-term): Shore Rd/Gosse Ct/Hope St east–west + Gazo Ave/Northview Dr/Fairfield Dr north, linking the VT 127 Path and Burlington Greenway through Schifilliti Park (PDF pp. 79–81).
- **High School → Greenway connection** via Institute Rd through North Beach Campground, timed to the high school reopening (M2) (PDF p. 81).
- Ethan Allen Parkway path (M3) and Plattsburg Ave side path (M4, incl. removing highway-style slip lanes at 127/Plattsburg) (PDF pp. 82–83).
- **Mobility hubs** at every focus area: transit + car share + secure bike parking + micromobility + bike repair + cafés; Citywide Mobility Hub Plan with a likely NNE pilot (M7) (pp. 13, 77, 85).

### Transit
- Route 7: restore Sunday service, increase frequency, shelters and benches at every warranted stop, dedicated transit funding tied to new development, legislative lobbying (M13–M14) (pp. 13, 120–121, 210–211).

### Transportation Demand Management (all seven launch Short-term, p. 17)
CarShare Vermont expansion (TDM1); outreach/education incl. bike workshops, e-bike test events at Ethan Allen Plaza, multilingual materials (TDM2); walk-to-shop trolley libraries (TDM3); Safe Routes to School at Flynn, CP Smith, Hunt, and Burlington HS (TDM4); mobility advisory committee (TDM5); annual mode survey (TDM6); volunteer microtransit for seniors (TDM7) (PDF pp. 122–129).

### New retail, services, and community spaces
- ~279k SF of commercial space across the focus areas: town center at Ethan Allen Plaza (Hannaford retained), village square retail at Plattsburg, restaurants/breweries/cultural venues at Ethan Allen Parkway, cafés at every mobility hub (pp. 15, 152–176).
- Community facilities: community center at Ethan Allen Parkway, senior housing + community center at Starr Farm, flexible civic buildings modeled on the Niagara Falls Exchange (pp. 144, 177, 183).
- Placemaking program (P1–P6, pp. 202–203, 218–219): North Ave design guidelines; Neighborhood Placemaking Committee (Quick Win); more community gardens; pollinator/native-plant habitat network (Quick Win); Plaza Activation Program (food trucks, concerts, art at Ethan Allen Plaza, Franklin Square, Ethan Allen Park); Gateway Arts Program (public art at the 127 ramps, Ethan Allen Tower, Franklin Square, Greenway access points, bus stops).
- Winter-month indoor community spaces identified as a missing place type to add (p. 194).

### Infrastructure
- North Ave water main replacement (UD8, prerequisite for development); green stormwater infrastructure corridor-wide; North Plant Stage 3 upgrade planned (not yet bonded) (pp. 52, 92, 188, 216).

## 5. Parks and Green Spaces Impacted

Context: the NNE has ~350 acres across 10+ parks — more open space than any Burlington neighborhood (p. 54) — but they are "destination" spaces; the neighborhood lacks integrated plazas and squares (pp. 54–55). The soft-sites analysis excluded protected wetlands and forests from development consideration (p. 68). Safeguard: **UD3 — any public open space developed for housing must be replaced with commensurate acreage/programming elsewhere** (pp. 17, 187, 216).

- **Burlington Greenway** — the recreational/transportation spine, full length of the waterfront. Gets its own framework (GW1–GW9): safety audit (Immediate Quick Win), wayfinding, year-round surface materials, better winter maintenance (path can't be salted — runoff enters the lake), eco-friendly wildlife-conscious lighting, recreation-waypoint mobility hubs, environmental education, adopt-a-path stewardship, possible widening of crowded segments (pp. 13, 130–135, 214–215).
- **Leddy Park** (75 acres) — gains a linear multi-use plaza connecting it to North Ave via the Ethan Allen Plaza redevelopment, "a welcoming gateway"; adjacent development "must complement — not compete with" the park; guided by the 2024 Leddy Park Comprehensive Plan (regional playground, BMX track, pavilion, fields) (pp. 33, 63, 66, 150–159, 198–199).
- **Ethan Allen Park** — most contested: the Ethan Allen Parkway focus area would put housing, a gateway plaza, community garden, and parking on/near park land. Residents opposed housing on the front lawn; realignment "does not require any change to Ethan Allen Park if that is not politically feasible"; some trees would be removed at the roundabout with replacement planting; historic 1905/1937 stone gateway could be relocated to the roundabout island. Also: front lawn is a pollinator-meadow candidate (P4), plaza activation site (P5), Gateway Arts at the Tower (P6) (pp. 104–108, 174–179, 203).
- **Starr Farm Park** — City-owned park partially developed under the Starr Farm West concept: senior/multigenerational housing plus new splash pad, ice rink, community gardens, soccer field upgrades, concession stand, Greenway mobility hub; playground, field, and dog park/community garden retained. Concerns: tree canopy and habitat loss incl. possibly threatened species along Starr Farm Rd (pp. 180–184).
- **Arms Forest & Rock Point** — explicitly preserved and protected; new pedestrian paths from North Ave through the Alliance Church site improve access; community concern about recreational pressure (pp. 28, 31, 33, 146–149, 201).
- **VT 127 interchange** — reimagined as "a park-like space" with regenerated native ecosystems, enhancing the ecological connection from Arms Forest to the Winooski Valley (PDF p. 102; p. 201).
- **Franklin Square** — activated as a public plaza with mobility hub, shelter, seating; plaza activation + gateway art site (pp. 196, 203).
- **Schifilliti Park** — Greenway Loop uses its recently completed path; double row of shade trees and benches proposed (PDF p. 80).
- **North Beach Campground** — the High School–Greenway connection runs through it (PDF p. 81).
- **VT 127 Path** — new connection to North Ave (preferred alternative approved by City Council May 2026), future widening, better separation from traffic (pp. 33, PDF pp. 71, 99).
- **Ecological network** — Key Ecological Connections link Rock Point, Arms Forest, the Intervale, and the lakeshore; mobility corridors double as ecological connectors with native plantings and rain gardens; pollinator habitat conversions proposed at High School frontage lawns, CP Smith rear lawn, Hunt Middle front lawn, Flynn front/rear lawns, the 127 ramp lawn, and Ethan Allen Park front lawn (pp. 66, PDF p. 79, 203).
- **New green spaces created** — new park + plazas at Plattsburg/Franklin Square, civic plazas at the Church site and Ethan Allen Parkway, linear plaza at Ethan Allen Plaza, community gardens at multiple sites, reclaimed slip-lane land at 127/Plattsburg restored to native ecosystems, pocket parks/rain gardens along realigned Ethan Allen median (pp. 63, 146–183, PDF pp. 83, 108).
- **Mount Calvary Cemetery** — community feedback supported its "parkification"; borders the proposed Plattsburg Ave path (p. 164, PDF p. 83).
